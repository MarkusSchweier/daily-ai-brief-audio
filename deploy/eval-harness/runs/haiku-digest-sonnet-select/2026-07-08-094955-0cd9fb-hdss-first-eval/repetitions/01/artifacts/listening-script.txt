SAFETY AND RESEARCH

Model collapse is a concern that's escalating. It's a degenerative process formally described in a 2024 paper in Nature by Oxford University researcher Ilia Shumailov and colleagues. The mechanism: AI models trained on data produced by earlier AI models progressively lose quality and diversity. The degradation is insidious because the early stage — when rare, minority, and creative outputs begin disappearing from the model's range — is invisible in standard benchmark scores. Only in late-stage collapse does overall performance visibly deteriorate.

The denominator is growing fast. By one estimate cited in published research, seventy-four percent of newly created web pages contained AI-generated text as of early 2025. Meta's Brand Memory feature and the broader Advantage+ system contribute to that contamination. Every AI-generated ad that runs on Instagram and Facebook, every synthetic image variant produced by Meta's creative automation tools, flows back into the shared ecosystem.

The good news: collapse is avoidable. When human data accumulates alongside synthetic data rather than being displaced by it, the risk shrinks. Aggressive dataset filtering, provenance tooling such as the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity's C2PA standard, and content watermarking are all real mitigation approaches.

The challenge is economics. These mitigations are active, ongoing costs — filtering pipelines to maintain, watermarks to embed, licensing deals to negotiate. Labs with Meta's financial scale can absorb those costs more easily than smaller or open-source teams. That's the equity problem baked into this risk: the largest companies can buy their way out; smaller labs and open-source communities face higher relative costs.
