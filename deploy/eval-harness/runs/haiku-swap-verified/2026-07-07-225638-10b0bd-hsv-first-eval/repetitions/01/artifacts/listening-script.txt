Your AI Brief for Tuesday, July 7, 2026.

Top story today: Anthropic has crossed a historic threshold. The company's annualized revenue has now reached 47 billion dollars, exceeding OpenAI's 25 billion dollars—the first time enterprise focus and direct API integration have outpaced consumer dominance in the AI industry.

This shift matters because it rewrites the competitive landscape. Anthropic did this by concentrating on enterprise customers and long-term integrations while OpenAI's ChatGPT, with over 900 million weekly active users, has generated massive consumer revenue. But the actual dollars show a different story. Approximately 80 percent of Anthropic's revenue comes from companies integrating Claude into their workflows; OpenAI remains more consumer-weighted. Claude Code, Anthropic's coding agent, contributed roughly 2.5 billion dollars to that total. This isn't just about valuation—it's a signal that the enterprise market will ultimately reward trust and customization over brand and first-mover advantage.

To fuel that growth, Anthropic inked a landmark infrastructure deal: a 20-year lease with TeraWulf for 401 megawatts of AI compute capacity in Kentucky. The deal is worth approximately 19 billion dollars in contracted revenue over its initial term. TeraWulf, a former bitcoin miner, is converting excess power sites into dedicated AI data-center campuses. The Kentucky facility begins delivering capacity in the second half of 2027 and reaches full buildout by early 2028. The deal alone exceeds TeraWulf's entire current market value of 12 billion dollars, illustrating how dominant frontier labs have become in dictating compute infrastructure economics.

Meanwhile, SK Hynix launched a historic IPO. The world's second-largest DRAM manufacturer raised approximately 28 billion dollars on Nasdaq—the second-largest share offering ever—driven entirely by demand for HBM3E, high-bandwidth memory, which supplies Nvidia's Blackwell GPUs. This is the bottleneck in AI inference: HBM controls how large a model can run at what speed. The IPO marks the moment when supply-chain criticality shifted from processors to memory. SK Hynix's public listing gives retail investors direct exposure to AI infrastructure supply for the first time.

On the product side, today marks a transition for Anthropic's Fable 5 model. Through today, July 7, the model has been included in Pro, Max, Team, and Enterprise plans for up to 50 percent of weekly usage limits at no cost. Starting tomorrow, Fable 5 becomes a metered service at 10 dollars per million input tokens and 50 dollars per million output tokens—exactly double the rate of Opus 4.8. Anthropic says this is temporary, pending capacity restoration, but the pricing shift immediately changes how developers route work. Fable 5 is the right tool for large code migrations, security audits, and multi-day autonomous tasks, but the cost incentive now shifts routine work toward cheaper models. Claude Sonnet 5, by contrast, costs 2 dollars per million input and 10 dollars per million output at introductory pricing through August 31.

The shift in developer choice is visible on OpenRouter, a popular API aggregator. Xiaomi's MiMo-V2-Pro has become the single most-used model by weekly token volume—21 percent of the platform's total traffic, ahead of OpenAI's 7 percent share. The model costs multiples less than frontier labs while delivering strong coding performance and a 1-million-token context window. However, unlike its Chinese stablemate GLM-5.2, which scores 62.1 percent on software engineering benchmarks and ships with open-source MIT licensed weights, MiMo-V2-Pro itself remains proprietary and closed-weight, available only through Xiaomi's API. Collectively, Chinese-sourced AI models now serve approximately 45 percent of all OpenRouter traffic, up from less than 2 percent a year ago. This is commoditization in real time: frontier labs compete on capability and trust, but volume adoption now belongs to players willing to compete on cost.

In enterprise news, Microsoft announced two moves in the AI space. First, the company laid off nearly 5,000 employees across Xbox and commercial sales divisions—continuing a broader tech industry trend of rightsizing after aggressive 2025 hiring. The timing suggests model-driven productivity gains are now reaching operational functions previously thought stable. Second, Microsoft launched a dedicated AI deployment company with a 2.5-billion-dollar commitment to enterprise adoption, signaling that enterprise integration is becoming core product strategy, not a side effort.

Finally, the United Nations closed its first-ever Global Dialogue on AI Governance in Geneva after two days of multilateral negotiations. The dialogue was informed by the first report of the UN's Independent Scientific Panel on AI, co-chaired by Yoshua Bengio, which warned that artificial intelligence capabilities are outpacing both scientific understanding and governments' ability to adapt. Bengio stated that the window for establishing effective global governance may not stay open indefinitely. The dialogue ran alongside the WSIS Forum and the International Telecommunications Union's AI for Good Summit, making this week in Geneva the most concentrated moment of multilateral AI governance activity to date. The consensus-based international approach contrasts sharply with the United States' emphasis on bilateral partnerships and voluntary self-regulation by frontier labs. How these approaches resolve will shape AI policy architecture through 2027.
